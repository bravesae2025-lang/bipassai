GPTZero Flagged My Essay as AI — How to Fix It

June 2026 5 min read By Bipass AI

Why GPTZero Flagged Your Essay

Seeing that red banner — "This document is likely AI-generated" — right before a deadline is a gut punch. But before you panic, understand that GPTZero doesn't read minds. It uses two statistical signals — perplexity and burstiness — to decide whether a human or a machine wrote your text. And it gets it wrong more often than most people realize.

Perplexity measures how unpredictable your word choices are. Low perplexity means predictable language, which detectors associate with AI. Burstiness measures how much your sentence lengths vary. AI text tends to sit in a comfortable middle range; human writing swings between punchy one-liners and long, layered sentences. If your essay scores low on both, GPTZero flags it — even if you wrote every single word yourself.

Common Reasons a Human Essay Gets Flagged

  • Formal academic tone: Structured, precise language mimics AI output patterns even when it's genuinely yours.
  • Repetitive sentence structure: When your sentences all follow the same rhythm and length, burstiness collapses.
  • Overused transition phrases: Words like "furthermore," "in conclusion," and "it is important to note" are extremely common in AI training data.
  • Non-native English writing: ESL writers often produce grammatically consistent, uniform text that scores as low-perplexity.
  • Over-edited drafts: Heavy polishing can accidentally flatten the natural quirks that signal human authorship.

How to Fix an Essay GPTZero Flagged as AI

1. Vary Your Sentence Length Aggressively

This is the single highest-impact fix you can make. Read your essay aloud and notice the rhythm. If three or four consecutive sentences feel roughly the same length, break one up or merge two together. Short sentences hit hard. Then a longer one can carry nuance, context, and detail that rewards the reader with something richer. That contrast is exactly what burstiness detectors are looking for.

2. Replace Cliché Transition Words

Swap out the phrases that dominate AI output. Instead of "Furthermore, it is evident that..." try "The data makes one thing clear..." or restructure the paragraph so no transition is needed at all. Some quick swaps:

  • "It is important to note" → "Worth noting here:"
  • "In conclusion" → "Taken together," — or cut it entirely
  • "Furthermore" → "On top of that" or reorder your points
  • "This essay will discuss" → Just discuss it

3. Add Specific, Personal Details

GPTZero cannot easily replicate the specific. A class discussion you remember, a statistic you tracked down, a counterintuitive example — these raise perplexity because the phrasing becomes less predictable. Weave in one or two genuinely specific observations per section. Not invented facts, but real ones you encountered while researching or thinking through your argument.

4. Read It Aloud and Edit for Natural Speech

Human speech is irregular. We use fragments. We restart thoughts. We occasionally repeat a word for emphasis. Reading your essay aloud forces you to notice where it sounds robotic and gives you an instinct for where to inject personality. If you stumble while reading a sentence, that's your signal to rewrite it.

If manual edits aren't moving the needle — especially under deadline pressure — a dedicated humanizer can rewrite the statistical fingerprint of flagged sections while keeping your argument intact. How to Test Your Fixes Before Submitting

Don't fix and submit blind. After editing, run your essay through GPTZero again and pay close attention to the sentence-level color highlighting — it shows you exactly which lines are still triggering the detector. Repeat the process paragraph by paragraph until the overall score falls below the flagging threshold.

Pro tip: paste one section at a time into GPTZero while you edit. This lets you pinpoint exactly which paragraphs are dragging your score down instead of guessing.

What NOT to Do

  • Don't run it through ChatGPT to "rewrite it more human." This almost always makes the AI score worse, not better — you're layering one AI output on top of another.
  • Don't add filler. Padding sentences with vague content lowers your grade and doesn't reliably improve your perplexity score.
  • Don't rely on GPTZero alone. Turnitin's AI module, Winston AI, and Copyleaks all use different models. Check more than one before submitting an important assignment.

Will These Changes Hurt My Grade?

Done right, they'll improve it. Varying sentence length and cutting stiff transitions are fundamental writing craft skills. Your argument stays the same — the delivery just becomes more dynamic. Many students find their edited essay reads significantly better after this process, not just for detectors, but for their actual reader: their professor.

The Bigger Picture

GPTZero is a statistical model, not a truth machine. It carries a documented false-positive rate, and researchers have flagged cases where passages from Abraham Lincoln speeches and the U.S. Constitution tested as "likely AI-generated." That doesn't mean you should ignore a flag — your institution may treat one as sufficient grounds for a review — but a flag is not a verdict. Fix the statistics, preserve your argument, and submit with confidence.
