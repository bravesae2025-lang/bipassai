Does Turnitin Detect ChatGPT in 2026? (And How to Actually Fix It)

June 2026 6 min read By Bipass AI

So you used ChatGPT to help with your essay. Maybe it was just to get started, maybe you were exhausted and had three other deadlines that week. Either way — you're now staring at the submit button wondering: will Turnitin actually catch this?

Short answer: yes, it probably will. But let's break down exactly how it works and what you can do about it.

Yes, Turnitin Has AI Detection Now (And It's Pretty Good)

Turnitin rolled out its AI writing detection feature back in 2023, and by 2026 it's become a standard part of most universities' submission workflows. If your school uses Turnitin, there's a solid chance your instructor can see an AI percentage score alongside the usual plagiarism report.

The detector isn't perfect — no AI detector is — but it's good enough to flag a raw ChatGPT output almost every single time.

Here's the thing though: most students think Turnitin is just checking for copied text. It's not. The AI detection works completely differently.

How Turnitin Actually Detects AI Writing

It Looks at Perplexity

Perplexity is basically a measure of how "predictable" your writing is. AI models are trained to generate the most likely next word in a sequence, which means AI-written text tends to be really, really predictable.

Human writing has a natural unpredictability to it — we use weird phrasing sometimes, we go off on tangents, we pick words that technically work but aren't the "optimal" choice. AI almost never does this.

Turnitin's detector measures this and flags text with abnormally low perplexity scores.

It Measures Burstiness

Burstiness refers to how much the sentence length varies throughout a piece. Human writing naturally fluctuates — short punchy sentences followed by longer, more complex ones. Then a fragment. Then something that runs on a bit.

AI text tends to be weirdly consistent. Every sentence is roughly the same length, with similar structure. It reads smooth — almost too smooth. That uniform rhythm is a major red flag for detectors.

It Recognises AI Writing Patterns

Beyond the technical metrics, Turnitin has been trained on enormous amounts of AI-generated text. It "knows" the patterns: the way ChatGPT structures introductions, the filler transitions ("it is important to note that…", "furthermore…", "in conclusion…"), the tendency to hedge everything, the absence of genuine opinion or voice.

These patterns stack up fast. Even a few paragraphs of raw AI output can produce a high detection score.

What Doesn't Work (Don't Waste Your Time)

Before we get to what actually helps, let's clear up some bad advice that circulates constantly:

  • Copy-pasting into a free paraphrasing tool — most of these just swap synonyms and produce text that's still flagged, just also awkward to read
  • Translating to another language and back — Turnitin is multilingual now; this stopped working ages ago
  • Adding typos manually — this is not a real solution and it will make your essay worse
  • Asking ChatGPT to "rewrite this to sound human" — it won't; it'll just produce more AI text with slightly different phrasing

The core problem is that surface-level changes don't fix the underlying statistical patterns that detection is based on. You need something that actually rewrites the text at a structural level.
