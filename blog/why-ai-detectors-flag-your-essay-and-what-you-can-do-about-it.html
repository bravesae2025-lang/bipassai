AI Detection

Why AI Detectors Flag Your Essay — And What You Can Do About It

June 2026 6 min read By Bipass AI

You rewrote it. You changed the words. You ran it through a paraphraser. And it still came back flagged.

Sound familiar?

The frustrating thing about AI detectors is they're not doing what most students think they're doing. It's not a plagiarism check — it's not looking for copied text. It's doing something more subtle, and once you understand it, you can actually do something about it.

Here's what's really going on.

What AI Detectors Are Actually Measuring

Tools like GPTZero, Turnitin's AI detection, and others aren't checking your essay against a database of AI outputs. They're analyzing the statistical patterns of your writing. Specifically, two main things:

Perplexity

Perplexity measures how predictable your word choices are. AI models are fundamentally prediction engines — they generate text by choosing the most likely next word, over and over. The result is writing that's statistically very "expected."

Human writers are messier. We use unusual phrasing. We pick words that technically work but aren't the obvious choice. We trail off, restart, use a metaphor that doesn't quite land. That unpredictability is what keeps perplexity scores high.

AI text has low perplexity. Detectors are trained to look for this.

Burstiness

Burstiness is about sentence length variation. Real human writing has a natural rhythm — short sentences, then long ones, then a fragment, then something that runs on a little longer than it probably should. That variation isn't random, but it's also not consistent.

AI-generated text is often eerily regular. All sentences are roughly the same length. The structure repeats. Everything flows evenly. Paragraph after paragraph, the rhythm stays the same.

That uniformity is one of the biggest giveaways detectors use. It's not something you can fix just by reordering sentences.

Other Patterns That Trigger Flags

Beyond perplexity and burstiness, detectors have learned to recognize specific writing habits that are very common in AI output:

  • Transition overload — phrases like "it is important to note," "furthermore," "in conclusion," "it is worth mentioning" appear constantly in AI writing. Real students use them sometimes, but not in every paragraph.
  • Perfect hedging — AI almost never takes a strong position. Everything is "it could be argued" or "some might suggest." Genuine opinion and voice are rare.
  • Absence of personal knowledge — real student essays often include specific examples, references to class discussions, slightly off-citations, or opinions that don't quite line up with the "official" take. AI is careful and generic.
  • Over-polished structure — every paragraph has a clear topic sentence, supporting evidence, and a wrap-up. Every time. Real student essays are messier.

None of these are instant flags on their own, but they compound. When an essay hits all of them together, the detection score goes up fast.

5 Practical Ways to Write Less Detectable AI-Assisted Essays

Here's the thing: most of this comes down to adding genuine human texture back to the writing. Here's how to actually do that.

1. Break Up the Rhythm Deliberately

Go through the essay and find long stretches where all your sentences are roughly the same length. Break some up. Combine others. Add a short sentence after something complex. Like this.

The goal isn't to make the writing worse — it's to add the natural variation that human writing has. A few intentional changes to sentence rhythm can shift the burstiness score meaningfully.

2. Kill the AI Transition Phrases

Do a find-and-replace pass specifically looking for:

  • "It is important to note"
  • "Furthermore"
  • "In conclusion"
  • "It is worth mentioning"
  • "As mentioned above"

Replace them with how you'd actually say it. "Also" is fine. Starting a sentence with "And" is fine. Just saying nothing and starting the next point directly is often fine.

These phrases are the fingerprints most detectors are trained on. They're easy to remove.

3. Add Specific Detail and Opinion

This is the hardest one but the most effective. AI output is almost always generic — it talks about concepts but rarely gets specific. Real student writing includes specific things: a statistic you actually looked up, a quote from the lecture, an opinion that's slightly more aggressive than strictly necessary.

Even one or two genuinely specific additions per section can break the pattern significantly.

4. Mess With the Vocabulary

AI tends to use the most "correct" word for everything. Find a few places where you'd naturally use a simpler or more casual word. Not everywhere — just a few spots. Real student writing mixes formal and informal language more than AI does, especially in argumentative essays.

If every sentence uses the perfect vocabulary word, it reads like a textbook. A few "basically," "kind of," or "a lot of" scattered through an otherwise formal essay actually makes it feel more human.

5. Read It Out Loud

This sounds obvious but it works. Read your essay out loud from start to finish. Anywhere it sounds weird or robotic — rewrite that sentence in the way you'd actually say it. Your spoken phrasing is naturally more varied and personal than AI output.

If a sentence sounds like something you'd never actually say to a person, it's worth rewording.

The Bigger Picture

AI detectors are getting better, but they're still probabilistic — they're making a guess based on patterns, not proving anything definitively. A high AI score doesn't mean you cheated, and a low score doesn't mean you didn't.

What matters is that your submitted work actually reflects your understanding and sounds like you. The best way to make that true is to actually engage with the content — add your own perspective, your own examples, your own voice. That's also, conveniently, what makes AI detection scores drop.

The tips above work because they're not tricks — they're just the things real student writing naturally has that AI writing doesn't. Put those back in, and the essay becomes genuinely yours.
